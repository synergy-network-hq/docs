This is an automated script that constantly monitors the compromised wallet. The instant it detects any funds, it immediately transfers them to the attacker's wallet. These bots are incredibly fast, often executing transfers within the same block that funds appear. If you try to manually move your funds to a safe wallet after you realize you're compromised, the sweeper bot will often beat you. You watch helplessly as your tokens disappear.Traditional recovery methods don't work in this scenario. You can't call customer service because there's no customer service in decentralized cryptocurrency. You can't reverse transactions because blockchain transactions are irreversible. You can't freeze your account because there are no accounts in the traditional sense. You're simply out of luck.The Self-Rescue Registry is designed to give users a way to save their funds even after compromise, by creating a time-based protection mechanism that you set up in advance when your wallet is still secure.Here's the concept. When your wallet is safe, before any compromise has occurred, you set up what we call a rescue plan. You designate a recovery address, which would typically be a hardware wallet or a cold storage address that you know is secure. You're telling the system, "if I ever need to rescue my tokens, send them here." You also choose a time delay. This delay can be anywhere from seven days to three hundred sixty-five days. This is how long the system will wait between when you initiate a rescue and when it executes.The critical third step in setup is granting an approval. You approve the Self-Rescue Registry contract to transfer your tokens. This is a standard ERC-20 token approval on Ethereum or a similar mechanism on other blockchains. By granting this approval while your wallet is still secure, you're giving the registry permission to move your tokens on your behalf later.This pre-authorized permission is what makes the entire system work. When your wallet gets compromised, the attacker has your private key, but they don't automatically have permission to revoke the approvals you've already granted. The Self-Rescue Registry's approval is already in place, creating a sort of first-mover advantage.Now let's walk through what happens in an emergency. Suppose you discover that your wallet has been compromised. Maybe you notice suspicious activity. Maybe your security software alerts you. However you find out, you need to act fast.You, or someone you've authorized, or even a trusted third party, calls a function in the Self-Rescue Registry contract called initiateRescue. This starts the timer based on the delay you configured during setup. If you chose a seven-day delay, the timer starts counting down from seven days. During this time, your tokens remain in your wallet. Nothing has been moved yet. This wait period serves two purposes. First, it prevents accidental rescues triggered by mistake. If you realize you initiated rescue by error, you can cancel it during the delay period with no harm done. Second, it alerts you that rescue is active, giving you time to take other protective actions if needed.Once the delay period expires, the rescue can be executed. Someone, again you or an authorized party, calls executeRescue. The contract then uses the approval you granted during setup to transfer your tokens from your compromised wallet to your safe recovery address. The tokens are rescued. The sweeper bot can't interfere because the contract's pre-existing approval lets it transfer tokens without needing your private key.Now, you might be wondering, why doesn't the attacker just revoke the approval to the Self-Rescue Registry? They could do that since they have your private key. The answer is that revoking the approval is itself a transaction that takes time and costs gas. If they do revoke it, that transaction appears on-chain and alerts monitoring systems that someone is tampering with your rescue plan. You see that revocation and can respond. You might immediately initiate rescue if it wasn't initiated already. Or you might execute rescue immediately if the delay has passed.Additionally, there's a cooldown mechanism. After you initiate a rescue, there's a ninety-day cooldown before you can initiate another one. This prevents an attacker from repeatedly triggering and canceling rescues to harass you or to fish for information about your recovery plans. Once a rescue is initiated, the attacker can't easily reset the clock.The self-rescue system creates a multi-layer defense. The first layer is the pre-authorized approval, which gives you a head start. The second layer is the time delay, which gives you a window to respond to compromise. The third layer is the cooldown period, which prevents rapid attack iterations. The fourth layer is the option to have trusted parties as authorized executors who can trigger rescue even if you've lost access to your primary wallet. In extreme emergencies, the contract owner, who would typically be the DAO, can pause the entire system. This would be used if a critical vulnerability is discovered that puts all users at risk.
